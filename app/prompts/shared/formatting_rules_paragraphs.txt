Response Format Constraints: "paragraph" elements

No other HTML tags other than <span class='font-semibold'></span> and <pre><code class="text-placeholder"></code></pre> (without classes) if you want to share code.

Response Format Constraints: "line_chart" elements
- Line charts must reflect realistic observability metrics and tell a compelling story. Avoid artificial flatness, symmetry, or over-polished shapes.
- The line chart's "title" should feel like a regular metric configured in the engineer's monitoring system, not a one-off bespoke chart for the exercise.
- Leave the "highlight_ranges" empty if there's nothing obvious to highlight in the visual.
- The timeline must align with earlier events in the conversation. Do not introduce conflicting time buckets.
- The chart should tell a story on its own. Flat lines or mirrored shapes are not helpful.
- If the data looks too tidy or symmetrical, add noise, spikes, or subtle trends to simulate real-world conditions.
- Avoid flat lines. At least one line must show noticeable variation.
- If using multiple lines:
- They should contrast. Do not submit two lines with the same slope or trajectory.
- The Y-axis values should be in similar ranges, so one line doesn’t dominate visually.
- If they overlap too much, either revise one or remove it entirely.
- It’s okay to use a single line if that’s what makes the story compelling.
- If two lines are closely related (e.g., cause/effect), introduce lag, overshoot, or jitter between them.
- If they rise/fall together, their shapes should still differ in slope, duration, or recovery profile.
- Good charts often show causal lag or offset between related metrics. If one signal rises steeply, the other should respond later or differently. Avoid mirrored rise/fall timing.
- Let the recovery period tell a story — use delayed falloff, noise, or instability to mimic real SEV fatigue.
- You should not hit the very top of the y-axis.
- Avoid mirrored bell curves — real systems rarely rise and fall in lockstep.
Examples of acceptable line shapes:
- A retry rate that spikes unexpectedly for 10 minutes, then falls.
- Two services that track closely until one diverges.
- A jittery CPU line over 20 minutes that reveals a subtle but clear rise.
- A cache hit rate slowly degrading, then rebounding.

- The element's JSON must include:
  - `"x_axis_labels"`: Array of strings, ordered labels (e.g. time buckets).
  - `"series"`: Array of objects with:
    - `"name"`: Series label (string)
    - `"data"`: Array of numbers, one per x-axis label.
  - Optional: `"highlight_ranges"`: Array of objects with:
    - `"start"`: One x-axis label (string)
    - `"end"`: One x-axis label (string)
    - Optional `"label"`: A label to show in the overlay (string).
    - The span between `"start"` and `"end"` should be minimal — ideally covering only 1–2 x-axis ticks — to avoid overlapping the entire graph background. Choose the smallest window that captures the anomaly or peak behavior.

  - Optional: `"title"` and `"subtitle"` (strings)


- The "content" field for line charts must be a Ruby hash structure, not a string or JSON string.
